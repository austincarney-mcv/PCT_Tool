Welcome to the Project Control Tool. This guide explains what the tool is,
what each section does, and how to use it effectively.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS THIS TOOL?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Project Control Tool (PCT) is a single master workbook that helps the
design team manage every aspect of a construction project from design through
to construction completion.

It covers:
  • How much fee budget is left (Cost to Complete tracking)
  • What drawings need to be issued and when
  • Whether all council/government approvals are on track
  • Client changes and their impact on fees
  • Design risks and safety obligations
  • Outstanding issues and actions

IMPORTANT: This is the master file. Do NOT overwrite it. Save a copy if you
need to experiment — the Cover Sheet reminds you of this.

Source file: 10162 - Project Control Tool.xlsx

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE SECTIONS EXPLAINED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. COVER SHEET
   What it is: The front page of the tool.
   What it contains: Project name, number, client, author, version and date.
   How to use it: Update the date and version number each time you make a
   significant change to the workbook.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

2. DELIVERABLES SCHEDULE
   What it is: The master list of every drawing the design team needs to produce.
   What it contains: Drawing numbers, titles, and scales for Architecture,
   Civil, Structural, and other disciplines. It also tracks which issue round
   each drawing is included in.
   
   The 5 issue rounds are:
     Issue 1 (60%) — Design Development         ~ 18 Sep 2025
     Issue 2 (70%) — Coordination               ~ 25 Sep 2025
     Issue 3 (80%) — BA & Procurement           ~  2 Oct 2025
     Issue 4 (90%) — Final Coordination/IFC     ~  6 Nov 2025
     Issue 5 (100%) — Final IFC (For Construction) ~ 20 Nov 2025

   How to use it: Tick off drawings as they are issued. Use the % Complete
   and Residual % columns to track progress against each issue round.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

3. COST TO COMPLETE (C2C) — DESIGN PHASE
   Sheets: "Cost to Complete - Start", "C2C Week 2" through "C2C Week 12"

   What it is: The financial heartbeat of the project. Updated every week,
   it shows how much fee budget has been spent, how much is left, and whether
   the project is tracking over or under budget.

   What it contains:
     - Team members and their hourly rates
     - How many hours each person is expected to work each remaining week
       (entered as a fraction — e.g. 0.5 means half a week)
     - Automatic calculation of total hours and cost to complete
     - A summary showing the fee balance (Under/Over)

   Key terms:
     Agreed Fee       — The total fee agreed with the client for this phase
     Cost at Close    — What was actually spent in previous phases
     Net to Carry     — The surplus or deficit carried forward
     Cost to Complete — What it will cost to finish the remaining work
     Under/Over       — Positive = under budget. Negative = over budget.

   How to use it: At the start of each week, create a new snapshot by
   duplicating the current week's sheet. Update the utilisation fractions
   for each team member to reflect actual planned work for the coming weeks.
   Do NOT go back and edit previous weeks — they are a historical record.

   Disciplines tracked: Architecture, Civil, Structural, Hydraulics,
   Landscaping, Certifier, Fire Engineering.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4. COST TO COMPLETE — CONSTRUCTION SERVICES PHASE
   Sheets: "CS C2C Week 1" through "CS C2C Week 12"

   What it is: The same fee tracking system, but for the construction
   administration phase (on-site stage).
   
   Timeline: December 2026 — May 2027 (Weeks 1–21)
   
   How to use it: Same approach as the design C2C. These sheets are currently
   templates — populate them when construction services commence.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

5. APPROVALS TRACKER
   What it is: A checklist of every government and authority approval the
   project needs before and during construction.

   Approvals tracked include:
     • Development Approval (Council — Planning Act)
     • Operational Works — Earthworks and Stormwater
     • Building Approval (Certifier — Building Act/NCC/QDC)
     • QFES (Queensland Fire & Emergency Services)
     • QLEAVE (portable long service leave levy)
     • Build Over Sewer/Stormwater approvals
     • Certificate of Occupancy
     • Plumbing Approval
     • Unity Water — Water & Sewer Connections
     • NBN & Telstra connections

   How to use it: When an application is lodged, fill in the Application ID,
   date lodged, and current status. Update the RFI Date if the authority
   comes back with questions. Mark as Complete when the approval is received.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

6. CRITICAL ITEMS REGISTER
   What it is: A running log of high-priority issues that need resolution
   and are critical to the project's progress.

   How to use it: Add a new row when a critical issue is identified. Assign
   it to someone with a due date. Update the Status to CLOSED once resolved.
   Do not delete rows — the history is important.

   Status options: OPEN | CLOSED

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

7. BRIEF COMPLIANCE REGISTER
   What it is: A checklist confirming that every item in the original client
   design brief has been addressed in the design.

   How to use it: Work through each brief item and mark it Compliant (Y).
   If there is a deviation from the brief, record it in the Deviation column
   and document the reason and any client agreement in the Comments column.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

8. DESIGN CHANGE REGISTER
   What it is: A formal log of all changes to the design after the brief
   was agreed. This is critical for managing scope creep and fee claims.

   Change types:
     Design Change     — A change to something already agreed/designed
     Design Development — A refinement or elaboration of the design
     Variation         — A change that may have a contract cost implication

   How to use it: Log every change, no matter how small. Record who requested
   it, the reason, and the fee impact across each discipline. Update the
   Status once the client approves or rejects the change.

   Status options: Approved | Submitted | Rejected | Yet to be submitted

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

9. RISK & ISSUE REGISTER
   What it is: A project risk log for tracking issues that could affect
   the project's time, cost, quality, or reputation.

   How to use it: Add risks as they are identified. Rate each risk for
   Priority (Low/Med/High) and Severity. Update Status as risks are managed
   or closed.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

10. RFIs (REQUESTS FOR INFORMATION)
    What it is: A log of formal questions from the builder/contractor during
    construction, requiring a response from the design team.

    How to use it: Log each RFI when received. Record the deadline and track
    the response. Note any Extension of Time (EOT) or Variation (VAR) implications.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

11. LESSONS LEARNT
    What it is: A record of things that went wrong (or well) on this project
    that should be remembered for future projects.

    How to use it: Add entries as issues arise. Be specific about the cause,
    effect, and what should be done differently next time. These entries feed
    into the firm's quality management system.

    Example already logged:
      Issue: Structural steel required 4-hour fire rating (Type A construction).
      Lesson: Identify fire rating requirements for steel early and get supplier
              advice before finalising member sizes.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

12. SAFETY IN DESIGN (SiD) REGISTER
    What it is: A legally required register (under WHS law) documenting
    hazards that have been identified during design and the actions taken
    to reduce risk for people who will build, use, and maintain the building.

    Risk Rating system (from the Risk Matrix sheet):
      Likelihood × Outcome = Rating
      Extreme / Significant / Moderate / Low / Negligible

    How to use it: Review the pre-populated hazard list at the start of each
    design phase. Add project-specific hazards. Record the design actions taken
    and update the Status as actions are completed.

    This register must be handed over to the builder as part of the tender
    and contract documents.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

13. VALUE LOG
    What it is: A record of value engineering ideas and savings that have
    been communicated to the client.

    How to use it: Add an entry whenever the team identifies a cost saving
    or value improvement. Record the date it was communicated to the client
    and whether it was approved.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

14. RISK MATRIX (Reference Only)
    What it is: The 5×5 risk matrix used by the SiD Register and Risk
    & Issue Register to calculate risk ratings.
    Do not edit this sheet.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TIPS FOR USING THE TOOL WELL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  ✔  Update the C2C weekly — don't let it fall behind.
  ✔  Use OPEN/CLOSED statuses consistently across all registers.
  ✔  Never delete historical rows — add new rows and update status instead.
  ✔  Keep the Approvals Tracker updated as lodgements are made.
  ✔  Log design changes as they happen, not in bulk at the end.
  ✔  The SiD Register must be reviewed at each design stage milestone.
  ✔  Save a dated backup copy at the end of each week.
